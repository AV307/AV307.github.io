Music Theory!

Welcome to the beginner's guide to music theory! Music theory might seem a little mundane and pointless, so before we start learning, here's a brief introduction to music theory.

Music Theory: A Brief History

  1. 500 BCE

    Ancient Era

    Ancient Greeks: Pythagoras studied acoustics and mathematics, applying his tuning method to music theory.

  2. 500 - 1300

    Middle Ages

    Monophonic Music: Gregorian chants were a large part of worship music, and technology allowed instruments to match the development of music theory.

  3. 1400 - 1600

    Renaissance Period

    Polyphonic Music: Polyphony became popular during the Renaissance, along with the development of notation.

  4. 1600 - 1750

    Baroque Period

    Tonal Language: The tonal system allowed composers to explore harmonic progressions, which led to the development of the current major-minor system.

  5. 1750 - 1820

    Classical Period

    Classical Harmony: Harmonic progressions became more predictable and phrases became balanced. Along with that, dynamic and expression markings were standardized.

  6. 1820 - 1900

    Romantic Period

    Expansion on Tonality: With the technical side of music theory being mostly ironed out, romantic music focused more on emotions than balanced structures, leading to more free and expressive lines.

  7. 1900 - present

    Modern Day

    Rejection of Tonality: Composers like Arnold Schoenberg and Anton Webern developed and popularised atonality and serialism, innovating on tonality and rhythmic structures.

Notes

Pitch

In music notation, the pitch of the note is determined by the position at which it is written on the staff. Generally speaking, notes written lower on the staff will have a lower pitch than notes written higher.

Note lenghts

In music notation, note lengths are represented by different symbols. These are the most common note lengths:

  1. Whole note (also known as a semibreve): a note that lasts for four beats in common time.
  2. Half note (also known as a minim): a note that lasts for two beats in common time.
  3. Quarter note (also known as a crotchet): a note that lasts for one beat in common time.
  4. Eighth note (also known as a quaver): a note that lasts for half a beat in common time.
  5. Sixteenth note (also known as a semiquaver): a note that lasts for a quarter of a beat in common time.

Intervals

Intervals might seem intimidating at first, but they're actually quite simple

As you can see, intervals are simply the distance between two notes on the staff. It doesn't matter what note is the starting point, all you have to do is count the number of spaces and lines combined between the two notes.

Foreign Language Terms

Here are some very basic italian terms, along with a few german terms, that are essential to be able to read and understand notated music.

Italian

  • Adagio: Slow
  • Allegro: Fast and Lively
  • Presto: Very fast
  • Andante: At a walking pace
  • Cantabile: In a singing style
  • Crescendo: Getting louder
  • Decrescendo: Getting softer
  • Forte: Loud
  • Piano: Soft
  • Fortissimo: Very loud
  • Pianissimo: Very soft
  • Staccato: Short and detached
  • Legato: Smooth and connected

German

  • Hauptstimme: Main voice
  • Lied: Song
  • Nebenstimme: Secondary voice
